==========================================================
 HOW TO RUN THE MORTALITY PULLFORWARD TOOL
 (step-by-step, no technical knowledge needed)
==========================================================

WHAT THIS TOOL DOES
-------------------
It estimates how much longer COVID survivors are likely to
live, based on the idea that COVID mostly killed people who
were already close to dying. You choose the assumptions;
the tool shows the effect on life expectancy instantly.


==========================================================
 PART 1 — START THE TOOL (the easy way, in your browser)
==========================================================

Step 1.  Open the folder that contains this file.
         (It is called "Mortality acceleration".)

Step 2.  Find the file named:  interface.html

Step 3.  Double-click it.
         It opens in your web browser (Edge or Chrome).
         Nothing to install — it works offline.

That's it. The tool is now running.


==========================================================
 PART 2 — USE THE TOOL
==========================================================

The INPUTS are on the LEFT (in a pane with its own scroll
bar — section headers click open/closed). Small (i) icons
next to the inputs show help when you hover over them. The
VIEW controls are at the TOP RIGHT, above the results.
Every time you change something, the results update
instantly.

ONE MODEL, TWO WAYS IN. The extra deaths COVID caused in
2020 and the deaths that go missing from later years are
two sides of the same coin — they must be equal. BOTH
sides are always on screen, and whichever one you edited
last is the input (it is marked "input"; the other side
shows live derived numbers in gray italics):
  - Drag the "Peak fraction" slider (this Part): the peak
    is the input, and the excess grid shows the cumulative
    excess deaths it implies.
  - Edit the "Excess by age band" grid
    (Part 2B): the excess is the input, and the peak
    becomes solved.
There is no mode switch — just edit the side you want to
specify. Switching never jumps the results: the side you
grab starts from its current derived values. Everything
else — the charts, the valuation year, the excess timing,
the tables — is the same either way.

Step 1.  Pick a scenario from the "Scenario preset" dropdown.
         Start with "Moderate base (recommended)" — it is the
         most defensible set of assumptions.

Step 2.  Look at the boxes at the top right.
         The first green box is the KEY output: the
         equivalent mortality multiplier — the single flat
         adjustment to the whole mortality table that
         matches the survivors' life expectancy (99.5% =
         a uniform 0.5% mortality cut). The second green
         box is the life expectancy gained by people still
         alive at the valuation year (2025 by default —
         change it at the very top of the sidebar). Other
         boxes show the pullforward peak and the cumulative
         excess deaths your assumption IMPLIES — compare
         that with the ~50% actually observed to
         sanity-check the assumption.

Step 3.  Choose whose numbers you want to see, in the
         "Output view" bar above the results:
         - "Focus cohort": set an age (for example 65) and
           click Male or Female — the boxes and charts show
           that person.
         - "Summary table shows": tick/untick Male/Female and
           edit the list of ages to control the table rows.

Step 4.  (Optional) Change the assumptions in the left pane.
         Assumptions are set PER SEGMENT (an age group, and
         optionally a sex). Pick which segment you are
         editing in the "Editing segment" dropdown, then:

         - "COVID effect active":
           untick to switch the COVID effect OFF for that
           group only — handy for looking at one group
           at a time.

         - "Peak fraction" slider:
           Of the people who would have died in the FIRST year
           after COVID, what share died during COVID instead?
           Higher = stronger effect. 0% = no effect.

         - "Grade-out horizon" (0–100):
           How many years into the future COVID reached.
           Deaths further out than this were untouched.
           0 = no pullforward for this segment.

         - "Shape":
           Linear = the effect starts at the peak and fades
           to zero at the horizon (halfway there, only half
           the peak applies).
           Step = the FULL peak applies to every year inside
           the horizon, then drops to zero. "X% of everyone
           who would have died within G years died during
           COVID" is Step with peak X% and horizon G.
           Exponential = it fades gradually and never quite
           reaches zero.
           (Careful with Step at 100% peak: if the horizon
           covers someone's whole remaining lifetime, nobody
           in that group survives COVID, so there is no
           survivor to measure — the tool shows a warning.)

         - "Apply these inputs to all segments" copies the
           current segment's settings everywhere.

Step 5.  (Optional) Change HOW the population is segmented,
         in the "Segmentation" section:
         - Tick "Separate inputs by sex" for separate
           male/female assumptions.
         - Type age cutoffs, e.g. "65" gives <65 and 65+;
           "65, 75, 85" gives four bands. Empty = one
           segment for everyone.
         - Click "Rebuild segments". The new segments appear
           in the "Editing segment" dropdown, keeping the
           settings they had where the groups overlap.

Step 6.  Scroll down for more detail:
         - Four charts showing the assumption and its effect
           (hover your mouse over them to read exact values)
         - A table with results for every age/sex you chose,
           including which segment each row used
         - "How the model works" — click it to expand a plain-
           language explanation of the math

TIP: There is no Save button and you cannot break anything.
     To start over, pick any preset from the dropdown again,
     or just close the browser tab and double-click
     interface.html again.


==========================================================
 PART 2B — THE OTHER WAY IN: SOLVE FROM EXCESS DEATHS
==========================================================

Editing the "Excess by age band" grid runs the
same model BACKWARDS from something observable: how many
extra deaths COVID actually caused. The moment you type in
any band (or click "Set all"), the excess becomes the
input and the peak becomes solved — the peak slider turns
gray/italic and shows the solved value. Same charts, same
tables; only the input side changes. (To go back, just
drag the peak slider or pick a preset.)

The idea: you say how large each age group's TOTAL extra
COVID deaths were, as a percentage of one normal year's
deaths for that group (50% = the excess equalled half a
normal year of deaths). All of those deaths are treated
as happening in 2020. Since the people who died early
were going to die in later years anyway, the same number
of deaths must be MISSING from 2021 onward — and they go
missing from the group AS IT AGES (a 60-year-old's death
pulled forward from 2023 would have happened at age 63).
Because death rates rise with age, the borrowed deaths
are a smaller and smaller share of each later year's
deaths — so the future effect is milder than the
headline excess number suggests.

The tool then SOLVES the one unknown: the share of
2021's deaths that were pulled into 2020 (the "Pulled
forward from 2021" box). There are two SEPARATE toggles for how
this all grades away over time — one for the pullforward
(repayment) side, one for the excess (spike) side.

WHAT TO SET:

  - "Excess by age band" (this direction's own
    input): the excess for every 5-year age group, with
    separate Male and Female columns (rows = age bands).
    Orange lines mark where your segment boundaries fall.
    All cells start at 50%; edit any cell, use "Set all
    to (%)" for everything, or "Fill segment (%)" to fill
    only the bands covered by the segment you are editing
    (its sex column(s) only, if segments are sex-split).

  - Everything else is THE SAME controls as Part 2 and
    applies here too:
      * "Pullforward inputs" — the Shape (Linear / Step /
        Exponential) and Grade-out horizon (or decay rate)
        of each SEGMENT govern how the SOLVED pullforward
        grades away. The "Peak fraction" slider turns
        gray/italic and displays the solved value (the
        peak is what gets solved). Squeezing the horizon
        forces a higher solved peak; stretching it lowers
        the peak.
      * Scenario presets still work — their segments and
        grade-out shapes apply; their peak is ignored.
      * "Segmentation" — age cutoffs / by-sex split apply
        in both directions; unticking "COVID effect active"
        for a segment zeroes that group's excess.
      * "Valuation year" (at the very top of the sidebar,
        normally 2025): you are standing at this year. The LE gain
        and the table show the remaining effect for people
        still alive at this year.

  - "Excess timing across years" (its own sidebar section;
    applies in BOTH directions of the model):
    three buttons, completely independent of the
    pullforward shape — this only controls WHEN the
    excess deaths happened, not how they get repaid.
      All in 2020 (default) — the whole excess lands in a
      single year.
      Linear fade — spreads the same total over several
      years, fading to zero (e.g. 50% over 3 years = 25% /
      16.7% / 8.3%).
      Empirical — a bell-curve (Gaussian) fade fitted to
      the actual COVID pattern: each year j after 2020
      keeps 2^(-j^2/4) of the 2020 level, so relative to
      2020 the excess runs 100% / 84% / 50% / 21% / 6%
      across 2020-2024 (exactly half by 2022) and is zero
      from 2025. As shares of the total excess that is
      38% / 32% / 19% / 8% / 2%.
    Changing this only re-times the spike in the charts;
    the totals and the solved pullforward share do not
    change.

WHAT YOU GET (the same outputs in both directions):

  - Green boxes: the "Pulled forward from 2021" share
    (solved here; assumed in Part 2's direction), and the
    life-expectancy gain at the valuation year for someone
    still alive then. Plus the cumulative excess (assumed
    here; implied in Part 2's direction).

  - A chart of the death rate for your focus age from
    2010 to 2035: the smooth baseline (the mortality
    table plus yearly improvement) and the COVID line —
    a spike in 2020, then a few years BELOW the baseline
    while the borrowed deaths go missing, then back to
    normal. This is the clearest picture of how COVID
    bent the mortality trajectory.

  - Charts of the pullforward by year, the survivors'
    mortality vs baseline by calendar year, and the
    cohort's death distribution with and without COVID.

  - The all-cohorts table (ages there are ages IN 2020).

If a warning saying "Infeasible" appears, the excess you
typed is larger than ALL the deaths available to harvest
under the chosen shape — lengthen the horizon (or use a
slower decay rate for Exponential) or lower the excess for
that group. (This can only happen in this direction; an
assumed pullforward is never infeasible.)

The Python twin of the whole tool is excess.py — the
sidebar "Run it in the codebase" box always shows the
exact command for your current settings, e.g.:

  python excess.py --excess-all 50 --grade-out 7 --valuation-year 2025
  python excess.py --peak 0.65 --grade-out 7      (Part 2's direction)


==========================================================
 PART 3 — GETTING RESULTS INTO EXCEL (optional)
==========================================================

The browser tool is for exploring. If you need spreadsheet
files of the results, use the Python version:

Step 1.  Open the "Mortality acceleration" folder in
         File Explorer.

Step 2.  Click once in the address bar at the top of the
         window (where the folder path is shown).

Step 3.  Type the word   cmd   and press Enter.
         A black window opens. This is normal.

Step 4.  Type the line below exactly and press Enter:

         python scenarios.py moderate_base --save

Step 5.  Wait a few seconds. When it finishes, a folder
         called "output" appears, containing a folder
         "moderate_base" with CSV files. Double-click any
         CSV file to open it in Excel.
         - summary.csv is the main results table.

To save a different scenario, replace "moderate_base" with
one of: default, short_harvest, elderly_concentrated,
long_harvest, exponential_moderate, exponential_long_tail,
sickest_only, uniform_benchmark

Bonus: in the browser tool, the sidebar box called
"Run it in the codebase" always shows the exact line to
type for whatever settings you currently have on screen.
Click "Copy command", then right-click in the black window
to paste it.


==========================================================
 PART 4 — MORTALITY IMPROVEMENT
==========================================================

The tool assumes mortality IMPROVES by 1% per year at
every age (people keep getting healthier over time, as
they historically have). The results also show an
"Equivalent mortality multiplier": the single flat
percentage adjustment to the whole mortality table that
would produce the same life-expectancy gain the survivors
got. For example 92.0% means "survivors gained as much
life expectancy as if mortality were cut 8% at every age".

IN THE BROWSER TOOL: use the "Mortality improvement" box
in the sidebar — untick it to turn improvement off, type a
different flat rate, or click "Import CSV…" to load your
own rates. The "Templates" links download a pre-filled
file you can edit in Excel (save as CSV, then import it).
The equivalent multiplier appears as the second green tile
and as a column in the all-cohorts table.

IN THE PYTHON VERSION:

To change the improvement assumption, use the black window
(see Part 3 for how to open it):

  python sensitivity.py --improvement-rate 0.015
      ...uses 1.5% per year instead of 1%.

  python sensitivity.py --no-improvement
      ...turns improvement off (the old behavior).

To use your OWN improvement rates from Excel:

Step 1.  Type ONE of these and press Enter:
           python sensitivity.py --improvement-template 1d
           python sensitivity.py --improvement-template 2d
         (1d = one rate per age; 2d = a rate per age AND
         per calendar year.)

Step 2.  A file appears in the folder, e.g.
         improvement_template_1d.csv. Double-click it to
         open in Excel. Every cell is pre-filled with 0.01
         (meaning 1% per year). Edit the numbers you want.
         IMPORTANT: use decimals — 0.01 means 1%, do NOT
         type 1 to mean 1%.

Step 3.  Save in Excel. If Excel asks about keeping the
         CSV format, click "Yes" / "Keep current format".

Step 4.  Run the model with your file:
           python sensitivity.py --improvement-table improvement_template_1d.csv


==========================================================
 PART 5 — PUTTING THE TOOL ON THE INTERNET (iWebFusion)
==========================================================

The tool is one self-contained file with no server code,
so it works on iWebFusion shared hosting as-is. A ready-
to-upload copy already exists in this folder:

    deploy\index.html

Step 1.  Log in to cPanel. Either:
         - go to  https://yourdomain.com/cpanel   (or
           https://yourdomain.com:2083), or
         - log in at my.iwebfusion.net and open cPanel
           from there. Login details are in the iWebFusion
           welcome email (check spam if you can't find it;
           you can reset the password from the client
           portal).

Step 2.  In cPanel, under "Files", click "File Manager".

Step 3.  Open the  public_html  folder (the "Web Root").

Step 4.  Click "Upload" in the top toolbar and select
         deploy\index.html from this project folder.

Step 5.  Done — the tool is live at  https://yourdomain.com

TO PUT IT ON A SUB-PAGE INSTEAD of the homepage (e.g. if
the domain already hosts something): in File Manager,
create a folder inside public_html named e.g.
"pullforward", and upload index.html into that folder.
The tool is then at  https://yourdomain.com/pullforward/

WHEN THE MODEL CHANGES: deploy\index.html is a copy of
interface.html. After interface.html is updated, copy it
over deploy\index.html again and re-upload (uploading a
file with the same name in File Manager overwrites the
old one).

Privacy note: everything runs in the visitor's browser.
Nothing typed into the page (or any imported CSV) is sent
to the server.


==========================================================
 IF SOMETHING GOES WRONG
==========================================================

- Double-clicking interface.html opens Notepad or another
  program instead of a browser:
  Right-click the file, choose "Open with", then pick
  Microsoft Edge or Google Chrome.

- The black window says "python is not recognized":
  Python is not installed or not set up on this computer.
  You can still use the browser tool (Part 1) — it does not
  need Python at all.

- The page looks squashed or cut off:
  Make the browser window bigger, or press Ctrl and - (minus)
  to zoom out.
