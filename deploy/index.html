Mortality Pullforward Explorer

COVID disproportionately killed people who were already close to dying: deaths were “pulled forward” from the years just ahead. The pullforward and the cumulative excess mortality it caused are two sides of one equation, so you can drive this tool from either side — assume the pullforward and see the excess it implies, or assume each age group’s observed excess and let the model solve the pullforward. Either way it shows the bent mortality trajectory and what survivors gain in life expectancy, standing at any valuation year.

How the model works

COVID disproportionately killed people who were already close to dying, “pulling forward” deaths that would otherwise have happened over the next several years. The model asks: given how large that pullforward was, how much healthier is the surviving population — and how did it bend the mortality trajectory? It is computed independently for each cohort, defined by its age in 2020 and sex, in absolute deaths per person alive at the start of 2020.

Step 1 — The no-COVID baseline

The SSA 2019 period life table is anchored at calendar 2019 and moved through time with the mortality improvement scale — compounded forward for later years and divided out backward to 2010. Each cohort’s baseline path follows its own age/calendar-year diagonal:

qx(age, year) = qx2019(age) × Π (1 − MI)    Db(t) = Ab(t) × qx(age+t, 2020+t)

Step 2 — The pullforward, specified from either end

One conservation equation ties everything together: the extra deaths in 2020 must equal the deaths that go missing from later years — and they go missing from the cohort as it ages (a 60-year-old’s death pulled forward from 2023 would have happened at age 63). Because mortality rises with age, a fixed number of borrowed deaths is a shrinking share of each later year’s larger death count, which automatically mutes the future effect.

excess deaths  E × qx(age, 2020)  =  Σt≥1 peak × w(t) × Db(t)

The Pullforward source toggle picks which side you specify:

  • Assume pullforward — you set the peak (the share of 2021’s deaths pulled into 2020) and how it grades away, per segment (age band × optional sex). The model computes the cumulative excess this implies.
  • Solve from excess — you set each 5-year age band’s cumulative excess (% of one normal year’s deaths) and the model solves the peak. If the excess exceeds every death available to harvest, the solve is infeasible: the pullforward is capped at 100% and a warning is shown.

How the pullforward grades away, w(t), has three shapes (normalized so every shape equals 1.0 in 2021 — “peak” always means the same thing):

linear:      w(t) = max(0, 1 − (t−1)/G)
step:        w(t) = 1  for t ≤ G, else 0
exponential:  w(t) = e−rate × (t−1)  (fat tail, no cutoff)

Step 3 — When the excess deaths themselves happen

Independently of the harvest shape, the excess can land all in 2020 (default), fade linearly to zero over N years (x(j) ∝ N−j), or follow the empirical Gaussian fade x(j) ∝ 2−j²/4 calibrated to the observed COVID pattern — relative to 2020’s level: 2021 = 84%, 2022 = 50% (exactly half), 2023 = 21%, 2024 = 6%, zero from 2025 (shares of the total: 38% / 32% / 19% / 8% / 2%). Timing only moves when the deaths happen; the totals and the peak are untouched.

Step 4 — The COVID path, multiples, and life expectancy at the valuation year

The COVID path adds the excess and subtracts the harvest, year by year; dividing by the surviving population gives the with-COVID death rates and the mortality multiple:

Dc(t) = Db(t) + x(t) − h(t),   multiple(t) = (Dc/Ac) / (Db/Ab)

The multiple spikes to 1+E in 2020, dips below 1.0 while the harvesting plays out, and returns to exactly 1.0 once a linear/step horizon has passed (exponential only approaches it). Standing at the valuation year, the model conditions on being alive then and compares remaining life expectancy with and without COVID — the LE gain is pure selection: the frailest died early, so those still standing are healthier than the table says.

Mortality improvement (the baseline itself gets better)

By default mortality improves 1% per year at every age, anchored to the 2019 table. Both the baseline LE and the survivors’ LE include it, so the LE gain isolates the pullforward effect. Set a different flat rate, turn it off, or import a CSV of rates by age (1D) or by age and calendar year (2D) from the sidebar.

The equivalent mortality multiplier

A second way to express the survivors’ advantage: the single flat percentage applied to the entire baseline qx table (from the valuation year on) that would produce the same life expectancy the survivors have. For example 99% means “the survivors’ LE gain is what you’d get by cutting mortality 1% at every age, forever.” Found by bisection, exact to ~1e-6 years.

Reading the outputs

  • The two drivers are mirror images: the observed ~50% cumulative excess solves to only a ~10–13% peak, while assuming a 65% peak implies an excess of ~300% of a year’s deaths — toggling between them shows whether an assumption is consistent with what actually happened.
  • The effect is largest for older cohorts: more of their near-term deaths fell inside the pullforward window.
  • For linear/step harvest shapes, the multiple returns to 1.0 once the horizon (and any later-ending excess timing) has passed — no lasting health advantage, only selection. Exponential has no hard horizon, so it only approaches 1.0.
  • The math mirrors mortality_model/excess.py; the sidebar command reproduces the focus cohort exactly with excess.py (in the assume-pullforward driver, run it once per segment). The older scenarios.py / sensitivity.py scripts use the legacy projection-year engine anchored at 2022, so their numbers differ slightly from this page.
